Scrum vs Kanban Analysis

Assignment 1 — Comparison Report at TG-Code

Project Overview

This assessment involved producing a research-based technical report comparing Scrum and Kanban within TG-Code, a company specialising in business information systems. The report critically evaluated Kanban’s potential to enhance software quality, collaboration, and productivity, and provided SMART recommendations for integrating Kanban into an established Scrum environment.

Key Focus Areas

  • Comparison of Scrum vs Kanban in terms of communication, collaboration, and productivity.
  • Evaluation of Kanban’s strengths and weaknesses in software development teams.
  • SMART Recommendations for Kanban adoption at TG-Code to enhance quality and delivery efficiency.

Background & Context

TG-Code operates with over 150 software developers, primarily using Scrum. The CIO requested analysis of Kanban to potentially improve software quality and delivery, while the CFO raised concerns about productivity impacts. Research included real-world studies and key academic sources such as Oza et al. (2013) and Lei et al. (2017), as well as hybrid agile best practices.

Kanban Strengths & Weaknesses

  • Strengths: Visual workflow enhances transparency and coordination; reduces multitasking; flexible for maintenance and continuous delivery.
  • Weaknesses: Lack of formal structure may reduce predictability; success depends on team discipline and metrics tracking; may initially reduce velocity during transition.

Scrum Comparison

Scrum promotes clear sprint cycles and role accountability, but rigid sprint planning can delay bug fixes or urgent work.

SMART Recommendations for TG-Code

  • Specific: Introduce Kanban boards within existing Scrum teams to visualise WIP limits.
  • Measurable: Track lead and cycle time for six sprints to evaluate quality and throughput.
  • Achievable: Pilot Kanban integration in one team before full adoption.
  • Relevant: Align with TG-Code’s quality improvement goals.
  • Time-bound: Complete pilot evaluation within 3 months.

Conclusion

Kanban can complement Scrum by increasing visibility, improving workflow, and enhancing communication. A hybrid “Scrumban” approach is recommended, enabling continuous improvement while maintaining Scrum’s structured delivery framework.

Secure-by-Design in Scrum

Assignment 2 — Security Integration Report at TechSage

Project Overview

This assessment explored how Security Design Analysis (SDA) can be integrated into Scrum practices at TechSage, a Manchester-based software company. The goal was to embed secure-by-design principles into agile workflows without compromising flexibility or speed.

Key Contributions

  • Identification of challenges in integrating SDA into Scrum.
  • Critical evaluation of Microsoft SDL for Agile, OWASP ASVS, and Security Backlogs.
  • Development of SMART recommendations for secure agile adoption.

Integration Methods

Method Description Strengths Weaknesses
Microsoft SDL for Agile Lightweight, sprint-based security lifecycle Fits existing Scrum rituals Needs team training
OWASP ASVS Security baseline and checklist Comprehensive and scalable Requires prioritisation
Security Backlogs & User Stories Adds security as backlog items Enhances visibility Dependent on Product Owner awareness

Challenges

  • Agile’s focus on iteration limits early security planning.
  • Teams often lack security specialists.
  • Tight sprints deprioritise security tasks.
  • Security accountability can be unclear in cross-functional teams.
  • Compliance (e.g., GDPR) requires evidence of security controls.

SMART Recommendations

  • Run threat modelling every two sprints.
  • Assign Security Champions to teams.
  • Add OWASP ASVS Level 1 controls to sprint criteria.
  • Hold security retrospectives.
  • Provide quarterly security training.

Outcome & Conclusion

Embedding SDA in Scrum improved security awareness, compliance, and product reliability. Integrating security into agile ceremonies enables “secure agility,” balancing speed and protection. Agile security works best through ongoing, embedded practices rather than external audits alone.
